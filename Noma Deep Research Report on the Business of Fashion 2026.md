**Market Landscape** 

**Global and luxury-adjacent context** 

Global fashion’s near-term outlook is subdued: McKinsey projects the global fashion industry will again post 1   
**low single‑digit growth in 2026**, with ongoing macro volatility driving value-conscious behavior. Key operational pressure centers on inventory and margin management: by 2024, fashion companies’ **inventory outstanding days reached all‑time highs**, increasing warehousing and logistics costs and 

2   
raising the stakes of assortment discipline.  

For premium streetwear, “luxury adjacency” matters: Bain reports personal luxury goods reached **€364B in 2024** and are forecast at **€358B in 2025** (mild erosion), reinforcing the importance of perceived value and   
10   
desirability—not just price elevation.  

**United States demand baseline and consumer spending signals** 

The United States remains a massive apparel/footwear market by consumption. BEA/FRED data show U.S. PCE at **$437.818B for clothing (2024)** and **$116.174B for footwear (2024)**, and **$553.992B for clothing/** 

11   
**footwear/related services (2024)**.  

However, consumer budget allocation for apparel is modest at the household level. BLS Consumer Expenditures reports average annual spend on **“Apparel and services” at $2,001 in 2024** (across all U.S. consumer units), while total average annual expenditures were $78,535. For a premium streetwear   
12 

label, this supports a strategy of capturing a **share of a small category wallet** by becoming one of a customer’s few “default” brands (repeatable silhouettes, consistent quality, clear values). 

**NYC-specific context that impacts marketing and operations** 

NYC is both a brand engine and a cost/compliance environment. NYDOL estimates that in 2022 (narrow definition) New York’s core fashion industries employed **130,170 workers** and paid **\~$8.8B in wages**, 

9   
indicating meaningful density of talent and suppliers (and intense competition for labor).  

NYC also has sales tax nuances that matter for pricing strategies below $110. New York State exempts clothing/footwear sold for **less than $110 per item/pair** from the **state 4% sales tax** (localities may vary), and NYC311 summarizes the **$110 threshold** and that items at or above $110 are subject to the full NYC rate. For premium streetwear, most products will exceed $110, but entry accessories/tees can be   
13 

intentionally priced to optimize perceived value and checkout friction.

**Supply chain and operations** 

**Manufacturing \+ sourcing posture (recommendation)** \- Start with 1–2 reliable factories per category (e.g., cut-and-sew fleece/jersey; bottoms) and add complexity slowly. 

\- Build a landed-cost model with tariff risk buffers. State of Fashion 2026 quantifies tariff turbulence and emphasizes mitigation actions such as shifting production, reducing assortment, and optimizing logistics. 8 

\- Implement QC gates early (proto approval → size set → PP sample → inline QC → final inspection). Inventory and margin pressure in 2026 makes “quality failures” more damaging because markdown risk is 

higher.

\- If a SKU misses milestone: trigger creative refresh, bundling strategy, or controlled markdown plan (avoid 11  
blanket discounts).
